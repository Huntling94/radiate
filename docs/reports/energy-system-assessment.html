Executive Summary

Situation

  • Radiate's simulation engine uses generalised Lotka-Volterra equations to model multi-species population dynamics across a 96-biome world.
    • Population growth is governed by a carrying capacity (K=500 per biome), trait-derived interaction coefficients, and temperature-based fitness modifiers
    • The model produces plausible predator-prey oscillations and competitive exclusion
  • The current model has no concept of energy. Carrying capacity is a static number, identical across all habitable biomes regardless of biome type, elevation, or moisture.
    • A desert supports the same K=500 as a lush forest — biologically implausible and strategically uninteresting
    • Producers, herbivores, and predators all compete against the same K, with no trophic energy budget constraining how much life a biome can sustain
  • Terrain sculpting (BRF-012) now lets the player reshape elevation and moisture, which changes biome types. This creates a natural demand for biome type to matter — the player expects that creating a forest should produce more life than creating a desert.

Complication

  • Simply varying K by biome type is insufficient. It would make forests "better" than deserts but would not model why — there is no causal chain from sunlight to plant biomass to herbivore support.
    • Without energy flow, the simulation cannot produce genuinely emergent trophic cascades — e.g., removing all herbivores should cause producer overgrowth and predator starvation, but the current model does not propagate these effects through energy budgets
  • The engine rework must not entangle with the planned Babylon.js UX migration (DDR-012). The owner explicitly requires engine and rendering work to be separated.
    • Energy system changes are pure engine (src/engine/) — no Three.js dependency, fully testable headless
    • The Babylon.js migration is purely rendering (src/world3d/) — no simulation logic
  • Four candidate architectures exist, ranging from minimal enhancement to full replacement of the population dynamics model. Each carries different trade-offs in realism, legibility, implementation cost, and tuning difficulty.

Resolution

  • The panel recommends Approach B: Hybrid Lotka-Volterra with Energy-Derived Carrying Capacity (unanimous, confidence 8.5/10).
    • Retain the proven Lotka-Volterra population solver but replace the static K=500 with a dynamic carrying capacity derived from trophic energy flow
    • Solar energy flows into biomes based on type (forests produce more than deserts), is consumed by producers, transferred to herbivores, and then to predators — each level constrained by the energy available below it
    • This creates a causal chain the player can reason about: sculpt terrain to create forest → more plant energy → more herbivore carrying capacity → more predator support
  • The architecture preserves the existing simulation contract. WorldState shape changes are additive (new fields on Biome, new energy-related traits). All existing tests remain valid. The Lotka-Volterra solver is refactored, not replaced.
    • Estimated implementation: 2 sub-briefs — (1) biome energy production + producer carrying capacity, (2) trophic transfer to consumers
    • Estimated test additions: 15-20 new engine tests
  • Approach A (pure energy flow) was rejected despite higher ecological realism — it replaces the entire population dynamics model, introducing significant tuning risk and regression potential.
    • Approach C (Metabolic Theory of Ecology) was rejected as over-engineered — the allometric scaling laws add complexity that is invisible to the player
    • Approach D (agent-based energy) was rejected as premature — it requires individual-level simulation (DDR-011) which has a ~500x computational cost increase

Current State Analysis

How the simulation works today

The engine runs a generalised Lotka-Volterra competition model (Volterra, 1926; Lotka, 1932) with Euler integration, stochastic noise, and sub-tick stepping for numerical stability. The core equation per species i in biome b:

dPi/dt = ri × Pi × (1 − Σj(aij × Pj) / Kb) + noise

Where ri = growth rate (from genome traits × temperature fitness), aij = interaction coefficient (competition, predation), Kb = carrying capacity of biome b, noise = stochastic perturbation.

Key parameters: BASE_GROWTH_RATE = 0.15, K = 500 for all habitable biomes, PREDATION_BENEFIT = −0.8, PREDATION_COST = 0.6, SELF_COMPETITION = 1.0.

What works well

  • Predator-prey oscillations emerge naturally from the interaction coefficients
  • Competitive exclusion (Gause's principle) works — niche overlap drives weaker competitors extinct
  • Speciation occurs via genome drift, producing evolutionary branching
  • Temperature-based fitness modifiers create climate-adapted species

What is missing

Gap Consequence Player Impact
No energy differentiation by biome type Desert and forest both support K=500. Mountain and ocean have K=0. No gradient. Terrain sculpting feels arbitrary — creating a forest does not measurably benefit the ecosystem
No trophic energy budget Herbivores and predators are not constrained by the energy their food source produces Removing all plants should crash herbivores, but the causal chain is mediated only by starvation checks, not energy flow
Metabolism trait is unused The genome includes a metabolism trait (index 4, range 0.1–2.0) but it has no effect on the simulation A trait visible in species cards that does nothing — undermines trust in the simulation
Carrying capacity is flat All habitable biomes are identical from the simulation's perspective The 2D biome map and 3D terrain show visual diversity, but the simulation underneath treats it as uniform

The concept of bottom-up trophic control — where primary production constrains all higher trophic levels — is one of ecology's foundational principles (Hairston, Smith & Slobodkin, 1960, "Community Structure, Population Control, and Competition", The American Naturalist). Its absence from the current model is the most significant gap.

Candidate Approaches

Approach A: Pure Energy Flow Model

Replace Lotka-Volterra entirely with an energy-budget system. Each species tracks energy intake, metabolic cost, and reproduction budget. Population change is determined by energy surplus/deficit rather than by the L-V equation.

How it works

  • Each biome receives solar irradiance (kJ/tick) based on biome type, elevation, and latitude
  • Producers convert solar energy to biomass with efficiency ηp (photosynthetic efficiency, ~1–5% in nature)
  • Herbivores consume producer biomass. Each individual needs metabolism × size0.75 energy per tick (Kleiber's law). Population grows if energy intake > metabolic cost + reproduction threshold
  • Predators consume herbivore biomass with similar energy accounting
  • Carrying capacity is emergent — it is the population that exactly consumes the energy available at each trophic level

Strengths

  • Most scientifically rigorous — energy conservation is a physical law, not a modelling assumption
  • Trophic cascades emerge naturally — removing herbivores causes measurable producer overgrowth
  • The metabolism trait becomes meaningful — high-metabolism species need more energy but may reproduce faster

Weaknesses

  • Complete replacement of the population solver — all 13 existing tick tests become invalid
  • Tuning difficulty is high — energy flow models are notoriously sensitive to parameter choices (Jorgensen & Fath, 2006)
  • Risk of "ecological death spirals" where slight parameter imbalances collapse entire food webs
  • Predator-prey oscillations require careful damping to avoid runaway boom-bust

Energy-budget models for ecosystems trace to Odum's "Energy Flow in Ecosystems" (1968). Modern implementations include Yodzis & Innes (1992)'s bioenergetic consumer-resource model, widely used in theoretical ecology but rarely in games due to tuning complexity.

Approach B: Hybrid Lotka-Volterra + Energy-Derived Carrying Capacity

Keep the proven L-V population solver but replace the static K=500 with a dynamic carrying capacity derived from trophic energy production. Energy constrains the ceiling; the L-V dynamics determine how populations behave under that ceiling.

How it works

  • Each biome type produces a base energy value (productivity):
    • Forest: 1000  |  Grassland: 700  |  Tundra: 300  |  Desert: 200  |  Ocean/Mountain: 0
  • Producer K = biome energy × moisture factor × temperature factor
  • Herbivore K per biome = (total producer biomass in biome) × transfer efficiency (~10%, following Lindeman's 10% rule)
  • Predator K per biome = (total herbivore biomass in biome) × transfer efficiency (~10%)
  • The metabolism trait modulates how efficiently a species converts available energy to carrying capacity — high metabolism = higher K but higher maintenance cost (lower fitness when food is scarce)
  • The L-V solver runs exactly as today, but K is now dynamic and trophic-level-specific

Strengths

  • Minimal regression risk — the population dynamics formula is unchanged; only K changes
  • All existing tests remain valid with updated K values
  • Immediately legible — forests support more life because they produce more energy; deserts are harsher; the player's sculpting has clear consequences
  • Trophic cascades emerge from K dependencies — removing herbivores raises producer K (less consumption) but crashes predator K
  • Metabolism trait becomes meaningful without rewriting the growth model

Weaknesses

  • Energy is not strictly conserved — K is derived from energy but populations aren't energy-tracked
  • Less scientifically rigorous than Approach A — it is an approximation, not a first-principles model
  • The "10% transfer rule" is a simplification (Cebrian, 1999 showed transfer efficiencies range from 1–40% depending on ecosystem type)

Approach C: Metabolic Theory of Ecology (MTE)

Apply allometric scaling laws (West, Brown & Enquist, 1997) to derive population parameters from body size and temperature. Metabolic rate scales as mass0.75 × e−E/kT, where E is activation energy and T is temperature in Kelvin.

How it works

  • All population parameters (growth rate, carrying capacity, interaction strength) are derived from the species' size trait and the biome's temperature using MTE scaling laws
  • Large species have lower growth rates, lower carrying capacities, but higher per-capita energy needs
  • Temperature directly affects metabolic rate — warmer biomes have faster population dynamics

Strengths

  • Highly principled — based on Nobel-adjacent theoretical biology (West et al., 1997)
  • Automatically produces realistic body-size distributions and abundance patterns
  • Temperature effects are mechanistic, not parameterised

Weaknesses

  • Complexity invisible to the player — allometric exponents (0.75, -0.25, etc.) are not intuitive
  • Requires Boltzmann temperature scaling (e−E/kT) — adds activation energy constants the player cannot see or control
  • Over-engineered for an idle game — the scientific rigour adds implementation and tuning cost without proportional gameplay benefit
  • The MTE is controversial in ecology — Price et al. (2012) showed the 3/4 power law is not universal

Approach D: Agent-Based Energy Budgets

Give each individual organism its own energy budget. Organisms forage, consume, metabolise, and reproduce as individuals. Population dynamics emerge from individual decisions.

How it works

  • Each organism is an agent with: position, energy reserve, genome, age
  • Per tick: organism moves, forages (gains energy), metabolises (loses energy), reproduces (if energy > threshold), or dies (if energy ≤ 0)
  • No Lotka-Volterra equation — population dynamics are emergent from millions of individual energy transactions

Strengths

  • Maximum emergence — complex behaviours arise from simple individual rules
  • Natural connection to the 3D creature visualisation — each agent is a creature on screen
  • Enables within-species genetic variation (DDR-011)

Weaknesses

  • ~500x computational cost (DDR-011 estimate) — requires Web Worker or WebAssembly
  • Complete rewrite of the population dynamics — no continuity with current engine
  • Extremely difficult to tune — emergent systems are unpredictable by definition
  • Offline catch-up (a core idle-game requirement) becomes intractable — cannot fast-forward millions of agents
  • DDR-011 explicitly defers this to v0.3 or v0.4

Agent-based ecosystem models are well-studied. See Grimm & Railsback (2005), Individual-based Modeling and Ecology. The computational and tuning challenges are well-documented in DeAngelis & Mooij (2005).

Deep-Dive: Recommended Approach (B — Hybrid L-V + Energy)

Design Overview

The hybrid approach introduces a biome energy production layer that feeds into the existing Lotka-Volterra solver by replacing the static carrying capacity K with a trophic-level-specific, dynamically computed K.

Core principle: Energy flows downward through trophic levels. Each level's carrying capacity is constrained by the energy available from the level below it (or from solar input for producers). The Lotka-Volterra solver determines how populations behave within these energy-derived ceilings.

Energy Production by Biome Type

Each biome type produces a base energy value representing net primary productivity (NPP) — the amount of solar energy converted to plant biomass, which is the foundational energy input for the entire food web.

Biome Type Base Energy Real-World NPP Analogue Rationale
Forest 1000 Tropical/temperate forest: 1000–2000 g C/m²/yr Highest moisture, densest vegetation
Grassland 700 Temperate grassland: 500–1000 g C/m²/yr Moderate moisture, open canopy
Tundra 300 Arctic tundra: 100–400 g C/m²/yr Cold-limited growth
Desert 200 Hot desert: 50–300 g C/m²/yr Moisture-limited growth
Ocean 0 N/A — uninhabitable No land biota
Mountain 0 N/A — uninhabitable Too extreme

NPP values based on Bar-On, Phillips & Milo (2018), "The biomass distribution on Earth", PNAS, and Field et al. (1998), "Primary Production of the Biosphere", Science.

Carrying Capacity Derivation

Producers

K_producer(biome) = baseEnergy[biomeType]
                    × moistureFactor(biome.moisture)
                    × temperatureFactor(temperature)

moistureFactor(m)   = 0.5 + m          // range [0.5, 1.5]
temperatureFactor(t) = max(0.1, 1 - |t - 20| × 0.015)  // peaks at 20°C

This gives K_producer ranging from ~30 (cold desert) to ~1500 (warm wet forest) — a 50x range replacing the flat 500.

Herbivores

producerBiomass(biome) = Σ population of all producers in biome

K_herbivore(biome) = producerBiomass(biome) × TRANSFER_EFFICIENCY
                     × metabolismModifier(species)

TRANSFER_EFFICIENCY  = 0.10   // Lindeman's 10% rule
metabolismModifier(s) = 1.0 / (0.5 + traits.metabolism × 0.5)
  // High metabolism → lower K (needs more energy per individual)
  // Low metabolism  → higher K (more efficient, but slower growth)

Predators

herbivoreBiomass(biome) = Σ population of all herbivores in biome

K_predator(biome) = herbivoreBiomass(biome) × TRANSFER_EFFICIENCY
                    × metabolismModifier(species)

Metabolism Trait Activation

The metabolism trait (currently unused, genome index 4, range 0.1–2.0) gains a dual role:

Effect Mechanism Trade-off
Growth rate boost r = BASE_GROWTH_RATE × (0.5 + reproductionRate) × (0.8 + metabolism × 0.2) × fitness Faster growth when food is available
Lower carrying capacity K adjusted by 1 / (0.5 + metabolism × 0.5) Each individual consumes more energy, so fewer individuals are supported

This creates a classic r-K trade-off (MacArthur & Wilson, 1967) — high-metabolism species are "r-strategists" (fast growth, lower equilibrium) while low-metabolism species are "K-strategists" (slow growth, higher equilibrium). The player can observe this in the species cards.

Integration with Existing Engine

Key architectural insight: The change is confined to how K is computed. The Lotka-Volterra solver, interaction matrix, speciation system, genome mutation, and all other engine modules remain unchanged.

New module: src/engine/energy.ts — pure functions for biome energy production and trophic K derivation.

Modified module: src/engine/tick.ts — replace the static K lookup with a call to the energy module's K derivation.

Modified type: Biome gains an optional energyProduction field (derived, not stored in save — no migration needed).

Obligation to Dissent

Computational Ecologist — partial dissent

"Approach B is the pragmatic choice, but I want to flag that the 10% transfer efficiency is a gross simplification. In real ecosystems, transfer efficiency varies from 1% (large warm-blooded predators) to 40% (cold-blooded aquatic consumers). Using a flat 10% will produce unrealistic predator:prey ratios for species with extreme trait values.

I would recommend making transfer efficiency a function of the metabolism and size traits — larger, higher-metabolism species should have lower transfer efficiency. This preserves the simplicity of Approach B while adding ecological plausibility."

Panel response: Accepted. The recommended design includes metabolism-modulated transfer efficiency.

Game Systems Designer — conditional support

"I support Approach B on the condition that the energy values are visible to the player. If the player can't see why a forest biome supports more herbivores than a desert, the system is doing computation without creating gameplay. The species cards, biome tooltips, and UI must expose the energy chain.

I also want to caution against making the energy system too punishing. In the current model, a desert biome still supports K=500 — players who like deserts won't enjoy watching their ecosystems die. The minimum K for habitable biomes should never be zero; even the harshest desert supports some life."

Panel response: Agreed. UI exposure is out of scope for the engine brief (it belongs in the UX layer) but the engine must produce data that the UI can display. The minimum K concern is addressed by the moistureFactor floor of 0.5 — even a bone-dry desert gets K = 200 × 0.5 = 100, not zero.

Performance Engineer — full support

"Approach B adds minimal computational overhead. The K derivation is O(biomes × species) — the same as the existing biome population lookup. The Lotka-Volterra solver is unchanged. Offline catch-up and time-jump performance are preserved. I have no concerns."

Implementation Roadmap

The implementation is split into two sub-briefs to keep commits focused and allow incremental testing.

Phase 1: Biome Energy Production + Producer K (BRF-013)

Item Detail
Scope New src/engine/energy.ts module. Biome energy production by type. Producer carrying capacity derived from energy. Metabolism trait affects producer growth rate.
Files energy.ts (new), energy.test.ts (new), tick.ts (modify K lookup for producers), index.ts (export)
Tests ~8-10 new tests: energy production per biome type, moisture factor, temperature factor, producer K calculation, metabolism effect on growth rate
Risk LOW — additive change, existing population dynamics unaffected for consumers
Dependency None — can begin immediately

Phase 2: Trophic Transfer + Consumer K (BRF-014)

Item Detail
Scope Herbivore K derived from producer biomass × transfer efficiency. Predator K derived from herbivore biomass. Metabolism trait modulates transfer efficiency and consumer K. Starvation mechanics updated.
Files energy.ts (extend), energy.test.ts (extend), tick.ts (modify K lookup for consumers)
Tests ~8-10 new tests: herbivore K from producer biomass, predator K from herbivore biomass, transfer efficiency, metabolism modifier, trophic cascade scenarios
Risk MEDIUM — consumer K becomes dynamic and population-dependent, may need tuning
Dependency Requires Phase 1

Separation from UX Migration

All energy system work is pure engine code (src/engine/). It has zero dependency on Three.js, Babylon.js, or any rendering code. The DDR-012 migration checkpoint (Babylon.js) can proceed independently — the energy system changes WorldState data, and the renderer consumes WorldState data. These are orthogonal concerns connected only by the WorldState contract.

Appendix: Glossary

Term Definition
Agent-Based Model (ABM) A simulation where individual entities (agents) follow simple rules, and population-level behaviour emerges from their interactions. Computationally expensive but maximally emergent.
Allometric Scaling Mathematical relationships between body size and biological parameters (e.g., metabolic rate scales with mass to the 0.75 power). Foundation of the Metabolic Theory of Ecology.
Bioenergetic Model A model that tracks energy intake, metabolic cost, and energy allocation to growth and reproduction for each organism or species.
Carrying Capacity (K) The maximum population a biome can sustain. In the current model, K=500 for all habitable biomes. In the proposed model, K is derived from trophic energy availability.
DVF Assessment Desirability-Viability-Feasibility framework for evaluating options. Desirability = value to users, Viability = sustainability and maintainability, Feasibility = implementation cost and risk.
Euler Integration A simple numerical method for solving differential equations by stepping forward in small increments. Used in the tick loop to update populations.
Kleiber's Law Empirical observation that metabolic rate scales with body mass to the 3/4 power (mass0.75). Used in the MTE approach.
Lindeman's 10% Rule Ecological principle that approximately 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. A simplification — real values range from 1–40%.
Lotka-Volterra Equations A system of differential equations modelling predator-prey and competitive interactions. The foundation of the current Radiate simulation engine.
Metabolic Theory of Ecology (MTE) A framework that uses metabolic rate as the fundamental biological rate to predict ecological patterns (population density, growth rate, etc.) from body size and temperature.
Net Primary Productivity (NPP) The rate at which an ecosystem accumulates plant biomass. Measured in grams of carbon per square metre per year. Varies enormously by biome type.
r-K Trade-off Ecological strategy spectrum: r-strategists reproduce quickly in unstable environments; K-strategists reproduce slowly but dominate in stable, resource-limited environments.
Trophic Cascade An ecological phenomenon where changes at one trophic level propagate through the food web. Example: removing predators causes herbivore overpopulation, which depletes producers.
Trophic Level A species' position in the food chain. Producers (level 1) convert energy from sunlight. Herbivores (level 2) eat producers. Predators (level 3) eat herbivores.
Transfer Efficiency The fraction of energy from one trophic level that is converted to biomass at the next level. Typically ~10% but varies by ecosystem and species traits.
WorldState The central data contract in Radiate's engine. Contains all simulation state: biomes, species, populations, events, RNG state. Consumed by both the engine and the renderer.
